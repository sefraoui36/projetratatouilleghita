The Making of Confit Byaldi

It wasn't just a dish — it was a message. When Remy chose to serve Confit Byaldi to the most feared food critic in Paris, he wasn't playing it safe — he was telling a story.

The process began with a delicate piperade — a slow-cooked blend of bell peppers, onions, garlic, and tomatoes, all gently simmered until silky and sweet. This formed the base of the dish — rich, warm, and comforting.

Then came the layering. Using a mandoline, Remy sliced zucchini, Japanese eggplant, and Roma tomatoes into paper-thin rounds. Each slice was arranged with care, overlapping like the scales of a flower petal in a circular, hypnotic spiral.

To elevate the flavors, he prepared a drizzle of garlic oil and fresh thyme, gently brushing it over the vegetables before the dish went into the oven, tightly sealed to steam and roast gently — slow and low.

After baking, the vegetables emerged soft, glossy, and infused with deep umami. A final touch of balsamic glaze added brightness and elegance to the rustic harmony.

This wasn’t a traditional ratatouille. This was Confit Byaldi: minimalist, modern, and packed with soul. A humble dish transformed into art.

It reminded Anton Ego — and all of us — that the power of food lies in memory, in emotion, and in the quiet confidence of simplicity done perfectly.

Recipe crafted with love and passion. Enjoy!
